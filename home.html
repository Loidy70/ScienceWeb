Atomic Structure

An atom consists of protons, neutrons, and electrons. Electrons orbit the nucleus in specific energy levels.

Water Molecule (H₂O)

The water molecule consists of two hydrogen atoms covalently bonded to one oxygen atom. It has unique properties like high surface tension and polarity.

Acids and Bases

Acids release hydrogen ions (H⁺) in solution, while bases release hydroxide ions (OH⁻). The pH scale measures acidity or alkalinity, with values below 7 being acidic, and values above 7 being basic. Examples include hydrochloric acid (HCl) and sodium hydroxide (NaOH).

Electrochemical Cells

Electrochemical cells convert chemical energy into electrical energy. Galvanic cells, such as batteries, and electrolytic cells, used in electrolysis, are examples of such systems.

Thermodynamics

Thermodynamics studies the relationship between heat, work, and energy. The laws of thermodynamics govern how energy is transferred and conserved. Key concepts include entropy and enthalpy.

Waves and Sound

Waves transfer energy through space or matter. Sound waves are mechanical waves that require a medium to propagate. Wave properties include wavelength, frequency, amplitude, and speed. The Doppler effect occurs when there is a change in frequency due to the motion of the wave source or observer.

Optics

Optics is the study of light and its interactions with matter. Reflection and refraction are key concepts in optics. Lenses and mirrors are used in instruments like microscopes and telescopes.

Kinematics

Kinematics describes the motion of objects. It includes concepts like displacement, velocity, acceleration, and equations of motion in both one and two dimensions.

Magnetism

Magnetism is the force exerted by magnetic fields. Electromagnets are created by passing current through a coil, and Faraday's Law of Induction explains how changing magnetic fields can produce electricity.

Newton’s Laws of Motion

First Law

An object remains at rest or in uniform motion unless acted upon by an external force.

Second Law

The force acting on an object is equal to the mass of the object multiplied by its acceleration: F = ma.

Third Law

Every action has an equal and opposite reaction.

Vector Projectile Motion

Projectile motion occurs when an object is thrown into the air and is influenced only by gravity (neglecting air resistance). The motion can be broken into horizontal and vertical components. The key formulas are:

  • Range: R = (v₀² * sin(2θ)) / g
  • Time of flight: T = (2 * v₀ * sin(θ)) / g

Photoelectric Effect

The photoelectric effect occurs when light strikes a metal surface, causing the ejection of electrons. The energy of the emitted electrons depends on the frequency of the incident light.
